About the Cabo San Lucas Marina

The Cabo San Lucas Marina is one of the most dramatic harbor settings in North America. Surrounded by desert mountains on three sides and opening to the Pacific-Cortez confluence on the fourth, it hosts a fleet of sportfishing boats that rival anything in the world, alongside luxury yachts, party catamarans, glass-bottom tourist vessels, and the humble water taxis that ferry visitors to El Arco and the sea lion colony at Land's End. The marina boardwalk — locally called the Malecón — is lined with restaurants, bars, boutiques, and activity operators, making it an all-day destination that transforms at sunset into one of the liveliest waterfronts in Mexico.

The marina's reputation as the sportfishing capital of the world is well-earned. The confluence of the Pacific Ocean and the Sea of Cortez creates one of the richest marine upwellings on the planet, drawing marlin, sailfish, dorado (mahi-mahi), yellowfin tuna, and wahoo in numbers that consistently astound even experienced anglers. The Bisbee's Black and Blue Tournament, held each October, is the richest sportfishing tournament on earth and draws the world's elite anglers to these waters every year.

Beyond fishing, the marina serves as the departure point for virtually every water-based activity in Cabo San Lucas — making it the natural first stop for any visitor who wants to experience the ocean side of Baja.

Top Things To Do at the Marina

Take a Water Taxi to El Arco

El Arco de Cabo San Lucas — the natural stone arch at Land's End where the Pacific Ocean meets the Sea of Cortez — is the defining landmark of Baja California and one of the most photographed rock formations in Latin America. You cannot reach it on foot; water taxis depart from the marina dock continuously from early morning until late afternoon, charging approximately $15 USD round trip. The trip takes about 10 minutes and includes a pass through the sea lion colony at Playa del Amor. Most boats will stop while passengers photograph the arch and the sea lions at close range. The best light for photography is in the early morning when the sun catches the golden granite at an angle. The most dramatic conditions are on slightly choppy days when the Pacific swell creates white water beneath the arch.

Book a Sportfishing Charter

Sportfishing is the activity that put Cabo San Lucas on the map and it remains the most iconic experience the marina offers. Dozens of charter operators compete for business along the dock — from shared "panga" boats starting around $120 per person for a half-day to private luxury sportfishing yachts running $800–$2,000 for a full day. The target species depend on season: marlin and sailfish are present year-round with peak season from June through November; dorado (mahi-mahi) run strongest from May through September; yellowfin tuna are caught year-round with the best action from late summer through winter. Book at least 24–48 hours in advance during peak season (November through March) as reputable operators fill up quickly. Sunset Cruise by Catamaran

The Cabo sunset catamaran cruise is one of those experiences that sounds touristy and turns out to be genuinely wonderful. Multiple operators run departures around 5pm aboard large sailing catamarans, typically including an open bar, light snacks, and a pass by El Arco in the golden hour light. The combination of the arch, the sailing, the drinks, and the sunset over the Pacific creates an atmosphere that feels almost designed for celebration — and it is. Prices run approximately $65–$95 per person. Pirate Ship tours offer a more theatrical version with entertainment, cannons, and dancing for families and groups who want something livelier. Book through your hotel, directly with operators at the marina, or through online booking platforms.

Glass-Bottom Boat Tour

The glass-bottom boat tours departing from the marina dock are one of the best-value activities in Cabo San Lucas, particularly for families with younger children. The boats pass El Arco, the sea lion colony, and Playa del Amor (Lover's Beach), then anchor in the protected waters for passengers to look through the glass bottom at the marine life below — tropical fish, rays, and occasional sea turtles are commonly visible. Tours run approximately 45 minutes to an hour, cost $15–$20 per person, and depart continuously throughout the day with no reservation required. The marine life viewing is best in the morning before afternoon boat traffic stirs up the sediment.

Marina Dining and Nightlife

The marina boardwalk offers every category of dining from beachside taco stands to high-end waterfront restaurants. For seafood, Mariscos Mocambo serves some of the freshest fish preparations in town at prices that reflect its local clientele rather than its waterfront location. El Squid Roe is the classic Cabo nightlife institution — loud, crowded, and fun in a way that requires embracing the chaos entirely. For something more sophisticated, Sunset Mona Lisa at the Hacienda Encantada (a short drive from the marina on the Pacific cliffs) offers one of the most spectacular sunset dining settings in Baja, with cliff-edge tables and a menu that matches the view.

Getting There from Casa de Corona

The marina is just 10 minutes from Casa de Corona by car via Paseo de la Marina. Uber is reliable and inexpensive (approximately $6–10 USD each way). Street parking is available around the marina perimeter, though it fills by mid-morning on weekends. The marina boardwalk itself is fully walkable once you arrive — all major operators, restaurants, and departure points are accessible on foot within a 10-minute walk of each other.
